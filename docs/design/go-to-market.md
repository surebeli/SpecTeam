# SpecTeam Go-to-Market Strategy

This document defines the go-to-market motion for the current product wedge.

It is intentionally scoped to the PMF stage, not the long-term platform story.

## Objective

The goal is not broad awareness or enterprise scale yet.

The goal is to prove that a narrow set of AI-native product and engineering teams will repeatedly use SpecTeam to:

1. detect spec divergence
2. resolve decisions faster
3. keep humans and AI tools aligned around one source of truth

Go-to-market should therefore optimize for repeated design partner usage, not vanity reach.

## Product Positioning For This Stage

### Market-facing name

Use `SpecTeam` as the market-facing product name.

Use `SpecTeam` consistently across product, repository, and workflow surfaces.

### Category expression

Lead with a narrow category expression:

- AI-native spec review and alignment
- decision alignment for AI product and engineering teams
- the alignment layer for spec-driven teamwork

### One-line explanation

`SpecTeam helps teams detect spec divergence, resolve decisions faster, and keep humans and AI tools aligned.`

## Initial ICP

Focus only on the initial segment most likely to convert into repeated use.

1. technical founders and product-engineering teams with 2 to 10 people
2. teams already using multiple AI tools in planning, spec writing, or architecture work
3. teams managing PRDs, architecture docs, and proposals in Git-based workflows
4. teams already feeling pain from contradictory AI outputs or slow design convergence

Do not broaden beyond this ICP before repeated pull is visible.

## Wedge And Offer

The initial offer is not a full collaboration platform.

It is a tightly scoped workflow:

1. import or prepare conflicting specs or proposals
2. detect divergence
3. review one disagreement
4. align on a decision
5. sync that decision back into the team's shared source of truth

The GTM promise should stay aligned with that workflow and avoid promising full spec-to-execution automation.

## Core GTM Motion

The right motion before PMF is a design-partner wedge, not self-serve scale first.

### Stage 1 - Problem clarity and qualified outreach

Primary goal:

Find teams that already feel the problem.

Recommended outreach surfaces:

1. founder and builder communities already using Claude Code, Codex CLI, Cursor, or similar tools
2. direct outreach to small product-engineering teams with spec-heavy workflows
3. open source maintainers coordinating design proposals with AI assistance
4. GitHub demos and examples that make the divergence problem obvious

The outreach message should be direct:

`Your PRD, architecture doc, and AI-generated proposals should not drift apart. SpecTeam helps your team detect divergence, make decisions faster, and keep everyone aligned.`

### Stage 2 - Guided first-use activation

Primary goal:

Get the team to first value in one guided session.

Success means the team can complete this path quickly:

1. see conflicting inputs
2. detect divergence
3. review one issue
4. record one resolution
5. understand where the shared source of truth now lives

The first session should be treated as a PMF test, not just a demo.

### Stage 3 - Repeated real-world usage

Primary goal:

Convert first-use success into a second and third real decision.

Within one week of onboarding, each design partner should be pushed to use SpecTeam on a real workflow involving:

1. PRD versus architecture mismatch
2. conflicting AI-generated proposals
3. disagreement on scope or technical direction

The strongest signal is unprompted reuse.

### Stage 4 - Lightweight paid pull

Primary goal:

Identify which stronger workflow or visibility need is valuable enough to monetize.

The first paid motion should not assume a fixed enterprise packaging model.

Instead, test whether design partners will pay for one of these layers:

1. team dashboard and decision visibility
2. approval workflows and clearer reviewer roles
3. reusable templates and stronger onboarding support
4. shared history, audit trails, and decision traceability

## Acquisition Channels

Before PMF, channels should be selected for feedback quality, not raw top-of-funnel volume.

| Channel | Why it matters now | Expected outcome |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|
| Direct founder outreach | fastest route to qualified teams | design partner conversations |
| GitHub repo and demo scenarios | shows the problem with low trial friction | inbound curiosity and demo requests |
| Short technical content | explains spec drift and alignment pain clearly | narrative validation |
| Peer referrals from early teams | highest-trust source once usage is real | better-quality design partners |

Avoid broad paid acquisition until repeat usage is visible.

## Conversion Path

The early conversion path should be simple:

1. see the problem clearly
2. run one guided session
3. use it on one real decision
4. reuse it on another real decision
5. discuss a lightweight paid pilot if premium pull appears

Do not optimize for a free-trial funnel before the repeated-use loop is working.

## Messaging Priorities

### Lead with

1. detect spec divergence
2. resolve architecture and product decisions faster
3. keep humans and AI tools aligned

### Do not lead with

1. multi-agent collaboration infrastructure
2. MCP architecture details
3. enterprise deployment and private-cloud positioning
4. large platform packaging before the wedge is proven

## PMF-Stage Commercialization

Commercialization at this stage should be evidence-driven.

### Free layer

Use open source distribution, examples, and guided demos to reduce adoption friction.

### Paid validation layer

Test lightweight paid pilots with design partners instead of locking a full pricing grid too early.

Good pilot shapes:

1. paid onboarding and workflow setup support
2. paid access to stronger decision visibility features
3. paid team workflow templates or approval support

The goal is to validate willingness to pay, not to maximize short-term revenue.

## Metrics That Matter Before PMF

These metrics should stay consistent with the PRD and PMF loop.

| Metric | Why it matters |
|--------|----------------|
| First-contact comprehension | tests whether the market understands the wedge quickly |
| Time to first successful guided session | measures activation friction |
| Number of active design partner teams | measures qualified adoption |
| Reviews per team per week | measures recurring behavior |
| Open to resolved conversion | measures decision effectiveness |
| Resolved to fully-closed conversion | measures whether decisions land in practice |
| Number of teams reusing the workflow | measures real pull |
| Number of teams asking for premium capabilities | measures monetization signal |

## What To Avoid Before PMF

1. publishing aggressive revenue projections as if packaging is already proven
2. scaling awareness spend before the message and workflow convert
3. building enterprise-heavy plans before repeated design-partner pull exists
4. using GitHub stars as the primary success metric
5. promising a full execution platform before the product truly supports that promise

## Exit Criteria For The Next GTM Stage

The current GTM motion has worked when:

1. a meaningful share of design partners reuse the workflow on real decisions
2. teams clearly understand the product within the first few seconds of contact
3. the path from divergence to decision is measurably shortening
4. a repeated premium request appears across multiple teams
5. at least some teams show credible willingness to pay for the stronger workflow layer

Only then should the GTM motion expand into broader self-serve growth, team packaging, or enterprise sales.
