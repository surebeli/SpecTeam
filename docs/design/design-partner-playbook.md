# SpecTeam Design Partner Playbook

This document translates the PMF loop and go-to-market strategy into a repeatable operating playbook.

It is designed for the current PMF stage only.

The goal is to recruit the right teams, run better conversations, capture better evidence, and decide faster whether SpecTeam is earning repeated pull and willingness to pay.

## Objective

Use this playbook to do four things consistently:

1. identify qualified design partner teams
2. run outreach with a clear problem statement
3. conduct structured discovery and first-use sessions
4. convert product signals into weekly product and GTM decisions

## What Counts As A Design Partner

A design partner is not just an interested user.

A qualified design partner should meet most of these conditions:

1. has 2 to 10 people in a product-engineering workflow
2. already uses more than one AI tool in planning, spec writing, or architecture work
3. keeps important docs in Git or a Git-adjacent workflow
4. has recent examples of conflicting specs, proposals, or AI outputs
5. is willing to try one real workflow within a week, not just watch a demo
6. is willing to give blunt feedback and discuss what would make the tool worth paying for

## Partner Selection Criteria

### High-priority candidates

1. technical founders managing product and architecture decisions directly
2. small teams with frequent PRD and architecture changes
3. teams already complaining about contradictory AI outputs
4. teams with enough urgency to try a live workflow quickly

### Lower-priority candidates

1. teams that only want to watch a demo
2. teams without current spec or decision pain
3. teams that do not use Git-managed docs at all
4. teams seeking a full enterprise workflow suite immediately

## Outreach Message Framework

Keep outreach short and problem-led.

### Short message

`SpecTeam helps product and engineering teams detect spec divergence, resolve decisions faster, and keep humans and AI tools aligned.`

### Expanded message

`If your PRD, architecture doc, and AI-generated proposals keep drifting apart, SpecTeam helps your team detect the divergence, make one clear decision, and sync that outcome back into a shared source of truth.`

### What not to lead with

1. multi-agent infrastructure language
2. MCP internals
3. enterprise packaging
4. large platform vision before the problem is acknowledged

## Outreach Sequences

### Sequence A - Cold founder outreach

1. lead with one clear pain statement
2. mention one concrete example of spec drift or conflicting AI proposals
3. ask for a 20 to 30 minute problem-fit conversation, not a sales demo

Suggested note:

`You may already have this problem: your PRD says one thing, your architecture doc says another, and your AI tools propose different paths. I'm working on SpecTeam, a workflow for detecting that divergence and helping teams resolve it faster. If this is happening in your team, I'd like to compare notes and see whether the current workflow matches your reality.`

### Sequence B - Warm referral outreach

1. mention the shared connection immediately
2. describe the exact type of team that is seeing value
3. ask for a short working session if the problem already exists

### Sequence C - Inbound follow-up

1. confirm whether the team has current spec or decision drift
2. ask for one recent example before booking time
3. route them either to discovery first or directly to a guided session

## Qualification Questions

Use these questions before spending time on a deep session.

1. How many people are involved in product and technical decisions today?
2. Which AI tools are currently used in planning, spec writing, or design reviews?
3. Where do PRDs, architecture docs, and proposals live today?
4. Can you name a recent case where two specs or AI proposals contradicted each other?
5. How did your team resolve that conflict?
6. Did the final decision actually make it back into the source documents?
7. Would your team try a real workflow within the next week if the problem is real enough?

## Discovery Interview Script

The first conversation should diagnose the problem before selling the solution.

### Part 1 - Context

1. What kinds of decisions create the most confusion for your team?
2. Where do contradictions usually appear first: PRDs, architecture docs, or AI-generated proposals?
3. How often do you find that people and AI tools are working from different assumptions?

### Part 2 - Current workflow

1. What happens today when two specs conflict?
2. Who decides what is final?
3. How does the team know the decision has been synced everywhere it needs to be?
4. What is slow, annoying, or risky about the current process?

### Part 3 - Pain intensity

1. How expensive is this problem in time, rework, or wrong decisions?
2. What happens if the divergence is not caught early?
3. Is this pain increasing as you use more AI tools?

### Part 4 - Product fit

1. If a tool could detect spec divergence and help your team close decisions faster, where would that fit into your workflow?
2. What would need to be true for your team to use that workflow repeatedly?
3. What would make it too heavy to adopt?

## Guided First-Use Session Script

The goal is to reach first value quickly and observe real friction.

### Success path

1. prepare two conflicting documents or proposals
2. run divergence detection
3. choose one divergence worth discussing
4. review and align on a decision
5. confirm the team understands the output and the updated source of truth

### What to observe

1. Does the team understand the product in the first few minutes?
2. Is setup friction acceptable?
3. Do participants understand who proposes, approves, and finalizes?
4. Do they trust the recorded outcome?
5. Do they want to use it again on a real decision?

## Follow-Up Questions After First Use

Ask these within 24 hours and again after one real follow-up use.

1. What part of the workflow felt immediately useful?
2. What felt confusing or heavier than expected?
3. Can you name another real decision where you would use this next?
4. What would stop your team from using it again next week?
5. What stronger visibility, template, or approval support would make this more valuable?

## Real-Use Validation Checklist

Do not count a partner as validated until most of these are true:

1. they completed one guided session successfully
2. they used the workflow on at least one real decision
3. they returned for another decision without heavy prompting
4. they can explain the value in their own words
5. they can name one capability they would pay for if the workflow keeps working

## Willingness-To-Pay Probe

Do not pitch a full pricing page too early.

Test payment pull with lightweight questions:

1. If this workflow reliably shortened decision time for your team, would it be valuable enough to pay for?
2. Which part is most worth paying for: visibility, approvals, templates, or decision history?
3. Would you pay for a lightweight pilot if it solved that pain in your next project?
4. What budget owner would need to approve that spend?

### Good early pilot offers

1. paid onboarding and workflow setup support
2. paid access to stronger decision visibility
3. paid team approval or template features

## Note Template

Use one note per team.

| Field | What to record |
|------|-----------------|
| Team | company or project name |
| Contact | primary person and role |
| Team size | number of active product-engineering participants |
| AI stack | tools currently used |
| Doc workflow | where PRDs and architecture docs live |
| Trigger example | one recent divergence case |
| Pain intensity | low, medium, high with a short reason |
| First-use result | blocked, partial, or successful |
| Real reuse | no, prompted, or unprompted |
| Premium pull | requested capability |
| Payment signal | none, weak, medium, strong |
| Next action | follow-up step and owner |

## Weekly Review Cadence

Review all active design partners once per week.

### Weekly checkpoint questions

1. Which teams reused the workflow this week?
2. Which teams stalled after the first session, and why?
3. What friction repeated across multiple teams?
4. Which premium request repeated across multiple teams?
5. What is the smallest product or messaging change that could improve activation, repetition, or payment pull?

## Decision Rules

### Double down when

1. teams reach first value quickly
2. repeat usage appears in real projects
3. the same premium request appears across multiple teams

### Refine when

1. the problem resonates but the workflow feels confusing
2. guided sessions work but real reuse does not
3. teams like the idea but cannot fit it into their current document flow

### Stop or narrow further when

1. qualified teams do not view the problem as urgent
2. repeated usage does not appear even after friction drops
3. willingness to pay remains weak across otherwise qualified teams

## Immediate Operating Checklist

1. recruit the first 10 to 15 qualified teams
2. run a discovery call before or alongside the guided session
3. force one real follow-up use within a week
4. record signals using the same template every time
5. review patterns weekly and change only what affects activation, repetition, or willingness to pay
